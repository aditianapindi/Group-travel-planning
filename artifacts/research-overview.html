02

The Problem

Six pain points that compound into trip-killing friction.

  1. Information fragmentation — Trip details scattered across WhatsApp, Sheets, Docs, Gmail, Splitwise, Instagram saves. "Flight options get shared but scrolling back requires archaeological excavation."
  2. Budget conflict — 59% of travelers report tension with travel partners. Budget mismatches hidden due to social pressure → surprise costs → last-minute dropouts. The conversation that NEEDS to happen first is the one everyone avoids. [AAA + Bread Financial, 2026]
  3. Decision paralysis — 50+ messages just to agree on dates. Too many options + too many opinions + no structured framework = nothing gets decided. Multivoting makes groups 50% more likely to reach consensus vs. plurality voting. [UW, 2022]
  4. Organizer burnout — One person absorbs all cognitive load. 71% of arrangers find it stressful. 10+ hours per trip on admin. The role is fixed, not rotating — same person every time. [CivicScience, 2024]
  5. Passive members / flaking — "People don't give any input — then complain about what was chosen." Average smartphone session is ~1m10s. Any interaction >30 seconds loses passive members.
  6. Schedule coordination — "Incredibly stressful" across time zones and availabilities. No simple visualization of who can do what dates across the group.

Key Findings

  • The role doesn't rotate. Academic research confirms organizers self-select by capability and convenience, and the role persists across trips. [Fardous et al., 2019; ScienceDirect, 2021]
  • It's gendered. Women serve as "gatekeepers" in household tourism decisions. [ResearchGate, 2013]
  • Culturally recognized. KAYAK coined "MVP (Most Valuable Planner)" in 2018. The "Planner Friend vs. Venmo Friend" split is now a TikTok archetype.
  • Counter-intuitive: Longer planning windows (3-6 months) correlate with more stress, not less. [CivicScience, 2024]
  • Financial burden: Organizers front costs on personal cards, chase payments for months.

What Nobody Does Well

  1. Full lifecycle — plan → decide → book → travel → settle (always fragmented)
  2. Budget transparency — anonymous collection BEFORE planning starts
  3. Couples/families as units — every app treats groups as collections of individuals
  4. Passive member activation — nudges that require action, not just notifications
  5. Organizer burden distribution — no app assigns tasks to distribute workload
  6. Planning-phase timing — no app intercepts at the right moment in the planning cycle
The Real Competitor Isn't an App

The "good enough" stack — ChatGPT → Google Doc → WhatsApp — doesn't appear in any app store analysis but is increasingly how groups actually plan. One person prompts an AI, pastes the output into a shared doc, and coordinates in chat. Zero friction, zero cost.

It fails at structure, state management, and organizer burden — but it's the real baseline to beat. The product must be faster to start than prompting ChatGPT and immediately more useful than a shared doc.

The Startup Graveyard

~700 companies tried. ~300 died. Highest failure rate of any travel vertical.

5 Ways They Died

  1. Research-to-booking leakage — Users plan on your tool, book on Booking.com. Consumers visit ~38 sites before booking. Your planning tool is one of 38, and you don't get the transaction. [Expedia research]
  2. Planning is perceived as free — Google Sheets is free. WhatsApp is free. Revenue lives at booking and during-trip, not planning. Planning sits in the value chain's dead zone.
  3. Infrequent use = no habit — 1-3 group trips/year. Can't build daily engagement or justify a subscription on something used twice a year.
  4. WhatsApp gravity — Everyone is already there. Asking 6 people to download a new app is harder than dealing with WhatsApp's chaos. "Good enough" beats "better but requires effort."
  5. Group adoption is social, not product — Organizer loves the app, but 5-7 others didn't choose it. They check once, respond late, go back to WhatsApp. Competing with inertia, not other apps.

What Survivors Did Differently

CompanyRevenueStrategy
Splitwise~$25M ARRSolved a financial pain, not planning. Pain recurs during AND after trip. Doesn't require simultaneous group usage.
Wanderlog$1M ARR, 5 peopleStayed tiny. Monetized during-trip features. Affiliate commissions. Never tried to be a platform.
Holidify~$400KContent-first SEO, bootstrapped, near-zero burn. Agent marketplace, not a planning tool.
The Honest Assessment

None succeeded by being a "group trip planning app." The biggest risk isn't competitors — it's the category itself. We must explain why this won't be failure #301.

Our Thesis: C + D

WhatsApp-native entry + during-trip monetization. Sidestepping the patterns that killed predecessors.

Thesis C

Work with WhatsApp, not against it

Tool lives inside WhatsApp — bot, shared link, mini-app. Solves the adoption friction that killed most predecessors. India-native (WhatsApp dominant, Business API exists).

Solves: Adoption wall, WhatsApp gravity

Thesis D

Monetize during/after, not during planning

Planning free. During trip: offline access, live expense splitting, real-time itinerary. After trip: settle expenses. Follows the only proven revenue pattern (Splitwise, Wanderlog).

Solves: "Planning is free" expectation

D.1: Experiences Affiliate — A More Defensible Revenue Path

Rather than building a booking engine for hotels/flights (too hard for V1), integrate with Klook, Viator, GetYourGuide via affiliate APIs.

Why experiences leak less than hotels/flights:

  • No comparison-shopping habit — Nobody opens 38 tabs for a walking tour. Price is fixed, experience is unique to the marketplace.
  • Lower price sensitivity — $40 vs $42 tour doesn't trigger switching. $300/night vs $250/night hotel does.
  • Discovery is the bottleneck — For activities, the hard part is finding the right one, not the cheapest. A planning tool that surfaces relevant experiences IS the value.

Our strategic advantage:

A group planning tool knows what 6 people have already agreed on — destination, dates, budget range, preferences, group composition. This is richer intent signal than any individual search on Klook or Viator. The platform becomes a qualified lead source, not just a referral link.

Watch: Klook Kreator Shops

Klook's Kreator program lets creators build curated "shops" of bookable activities as packaged itineraries. If creators start selling packaged group itineraries with pre-selected, bookable activities, that's a partial substitute for the organizer curation role our product relieves. Doesn't kill coordination/voting features, but weakens itinerary curation value prop.

Unit Economics Concern

8% commission on a $40 tour = $3.20 per booking. For a group of 6 booking 3-4 activities: ~$40-75 per trip. At 1-2 trips/year per group, this is thin. Must model whether this scales or remains supplementary revenue alongside expense-splitting features.

Surprising Findings

  • Peer knowledge > search: Interviewee 7 wanted to talk to people who've been there, not browse sites. Over-budget on taxis because "that info was nowhere."
  • Food drives destination choice: Interviewee 3's group picked Lonavala partly for food options. Reviews failed them on arrival.
  • School calendars are a hard constraint: Ruchi (Australia) plans entirely around state school holidays — QLD, VIC, NSW don't align.
  • Trips end friendships: Interviewee 7 no longer travels with the 5 couples from their Austria trip. Still friends. But never again.

What's Next

Open questions, timeline, and what we need to validate before PRD.

Must Validate in Interviews

Adoption & Habit

  • Would friends actually download a separate app?
  • Is WhatsApp-native viable or too constraining?
  • How many group trips/year? Enough for engagement?
  • Do serial organizers plan for 2-3 distinct groups?

Revenue & Leakage

  • Would users book inside a planning tool, or always go to OTAs?
  • Would groups book activities in-context or go to Klook/Viator directly?
  • What would they pay for — and when in the trip lifecycle?
  • Would curated activity suggestions feel helpful or spammy?

Product Principles

  1. Organizer relief is the entry point — but the product must serve all participants
  2. Async-first — groups span time zones. Never require real-time coordination
  3. Transparency > control — everyone sees budget, options, votes
  4. Progressive complexity — weekend trip and 2-week international use the same tool
  5. Mobile-first — every flow must work on a phone in under 60 seconds
  6. Meet users where they are — WhatsApp-native entry before standalone app flows
  7. Capture value during/after the trip — not before. Planning is perceived as free
  8. Avoid booking leakage — or accept it — don't assume users will book inside your tool

The Core Bet

The risk of building a logistics tool when people actually need an emotional momentum tool — something that keeps excitement alive through the 2-3 week death zone. The product's job: keep pain below the breaking point where trips die.

WhatsApp-native entry (Thesis C) + during-trip monetization via experiences affiliate (Thesis D + D.1) = a path that sidesteps the 5 patterns that killed ~300 predecessors.
