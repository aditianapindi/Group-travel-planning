# Product Principles

These guide every product decision. When two choices seem equal, these break the tie.

1. **Organizer relief is the entry point** — but the product must serve all participants. If only the organizer benefits, we've built a productivity tool, not a collaboration platform.

2. **Async-first** — groups span time zones, schedules, and decision speeds. Never require real-time coordination to make progress.

3. **Transparency > control** — everyone sees budget, options, votes. No side channels needed. Trust is built through visibility.

4. **Progressive complexity** — weekend getaway and 2-week international trip use the same tool. Complexity should reveal itself only when needed.

5. **Mobile-first** — planning happens in stolen moments, not at desks. Average smartphone session is ~1 min 10 sec [Source: Google micro-moments]. Every flow must work on a phone screen in under 60 seconds.

6. **Meet users where they are** — WhatsApp/iMessage gravity is real. 77% of Americans travel with others [Source: Talker Research, 2025] but asking 6 people to download a new app is the hardest adoption problem. Consider WhatsApp-native entry points before standalone app flows.

7. **Capture value during and after the trip, not just before** — Planning is perceived as free. The only proven monetizable group coordination is expense splitting (Splitwise: $25M ARR). Features useful during the trip (offline access, live expense tracking) convert to paid better than planning-phase features. See `knowledge/decision-category-risk.md`.

8. **Avoid booking leakage — or accept it** — Users plan on one tool, book on another. ~300 startups died from this pattern. Either integrate booking (requires partnerships + trust) or embrace affiliate economics and build value elsewhere. Don't assume users will book inside your tool — validate this in interviews.

## Quality Bar

- Every feature tied to a validated user problem
- No feature creep — solve one workflow end-to-end before expanding
- Assumptions tracked explicitly and tested, not assumed true
- Competitor analysis = what they do AND what they get wrong
- Every quantitative claim must cite its source — no unsourced stats in any research or product document
